University of Kentucky Accelerator Lab

At the University of Kentucky Accelerator Laboratory (UKAL), I am developing a quasi-stellar neutron source using the 7Li(p, n)7Be reaction near threshold (Ep ∼ 1.9 MeV), producing a neutron spectrum that mimics a Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution. This source enables measurement of Maxwellian-averaged neutron capture cross sections (MACS) for key isotopes involved in the slow neutron capture process (s-process), which is responsible for creating most elements heavier than iron through successive neutron captures on stable nuclei. These measurements will provide critical nuclear data to improve models of stellar nucleosynthesis.

Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB)

At the Facility for Rare Isotope Beams (FRIB), I am advancing the understanding of nucleosynthesis in Type I X-ray bursts, focusing on critical reaction rates such as 15O(α, γ)19Ne, which governs breakout from the hot CNO cycle and the onset of the rp-process. I led FRIB experiment E21072, which utilized the Gaseous Detector with GErmanium Tagging (GADGET II), a cutting-edge detection system that I designed, built, and commissioned to measure the β-delayed (β p α) decay of 20Mg. By capturing the detailed 3D event topology, we determined a finite α-particle branching ratio, demonstrating both experimental innovation and my leadership in executing this complex measurement.
In addition, at FRIB my group will be conducting experiment E25046 using β-delayed proton spectroscopy of 58Zn to identify resonances in 58Cu, directly constraining the 57Ni(p, γ)58Cu reaction rate. This reaction plays a vital role in the synthesis of 44Ti in core-collapse supernovae, influencing energy generation and neutron star crust cooling. These measurements will exploit GADGET’s unique capability to detect protons in coincidence with γ rays, allowing unprecedented selectivity and precision in nuclear astrophysics studies.

Florida State University - Fox Lab

At the John D. Fox Accelerator Laboratory, Florida State University, I am conducting charge-exchange studies using the (3He, t) and (3He, d) reactions on 46Ti to probe mirror states in 46V and 47V. These data provide indirect constraints on the 45V(p, γ)46Cr and 46Ti(p, γ)47V reactions, both critical for modeling explosive nucleosynthesis.

Together, these efforts will significantly reduce uncertainties in key astrophysical reaction rates, enhance simulations of stellar explosions, and advance our understanding of how the elements in the universe were formed.
